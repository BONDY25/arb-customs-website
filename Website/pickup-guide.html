Guitar Pickups

How They Work

We all know pickups are an essential part of an electric guitar, but how do they actually work? The name gives a hint—they "pick up" the vibrations of the guitar's strings. To understand how, we need a bit of a physics lesson. Guitar pickups operate on the principle that when a ferrous material (something with ferromagnetic properties, meaning it's attracted to magnets) moves through a magnetic field, it induces an AC electrical current. This current can then be transferred to conductors inside the field.

By wrapping a conductor like copper wire around something that produces a magnetic field, such as a magnet, we can induce a pulse of current when a ferrous material passes over it. If we connect one end of the copper wire to an amplifier and the other to ground (usually integrated into the amp's input), we can amplify this pulse of current and send it through a speaker.

Now, when a ferrous material, like a guitar string, moves repeatedly over the pickup, multiple pulses are generated, forming a signal. The pickup itself is the contraption generating the magnetic field, the guitar strings are the ferrous material, and the amplifier is, well, the amplifier. When you pluck a string, the frequency of its vibration is converted into an AC current, passed through the pickup, into the amp and speaker, and finally to your ears. The harder you hit the string, the stronger the signal. There are many factors that influence this process, but it all starts with this basic principle.

Ready to dive deeper? Read on below.



Single Coil Pickups

When a conductor is wrapped around a magnet, it forms a coil. If you have just one of these, it's called a single coil. Single coils traditionally produce a bright, clear tone and are often praised for their ability to capture the dynamics of your playing. You can usually hear a distinct difference between playing lightly and softly versus playing hard. Because of this sensitivity, single coils can sometimes pick up unwanted noise, either through microphonic interactions or electromagnetic interference (which is emitted by most electronic devices).

This unwanted noise is especially noticeable when the amplifier is cranked up or being driven hard. Over the years, guitar companies have developed various technologies to combat this issue, creating solutions that retain the distinctive single-coil sound and dynamics while minimising noise and interference.



Double Coil Pickups (Humbuckers)

If we take two coils and connect them in series—where the positive end of one coil is connected to the negative end of the other—we create what's commonly known as a humbucker. This configuration cancels out electromagnetic interference and unwanted noise by putting the coils out of phase with each other, effectively "bucking" the hum. Humbuckers are renowned for producing a warmer, fuller, and thicker sound. Since there are two coils picking up the string vibrations, the output is generally stronger compared to a single coil. However, this can sometimes result in subtle playing dynamics being less noticeable.



Magnets

The magnet used in a pickup plays a crucial role in its function. The stronger the magnet, the more powerful the magnetic field it generates, and consequently, the stronger the signal the pickup produces. However, stronger magnets can often result in a brighter, harsher, or more brittle tone. Despite this, there are ways to counterbalance or manipulate these tonal characteristics to achieve a more balanced sound.



Conductors

The conductor is the material that carries the electrical current generated in the magnetic field, transferring the signal from the magnetic field to the output of the guitar. The quality of the conductor plays a significant role in how well the signal reaches the amplifier. Copper is the go-to material for most manufacturers because of its excellent conductivity. However, copper alone isn't enough—it must be wrapped around a magnet, as discussed earlier. The number of times the conductor is wound around the magnet significantly impacts the overall sound.

Simply put, the more wraps (or windings), the more conductive material there is within the magnetic field, which produces a stronger signal. However, too many windings can overcrowd the magnetic field, causing more low-end frequencies to be picked up. This can result in a muddy or dark tone. While a stronger magnet can help balance things out, there's a limit to how much can be done before the tone becomes compromised. Many high-output, "overwound" pickups manage to produce powerful tones without excessive muddiness.



Pole Pieces

Pole pieces are the visible components at the top of the pickup, usually one for each string. Made from ferrous materials, they are either in direct contact with the magnet or sometimes part of the magnet itself. Their primary role is to direct the magnetic field towards the guitar strings, ensuring the strings always pass through the field. While pole pieces are typically uniform in size across different pickups, some brands produce models with larger-than-normal pole pieces. These larger pieces focus more of the magnetic field onto the string, resulting in a stronger signal. However, similar to conductors, increasing the size too much can lead to a darker, muddier sound if overdone.



Active & Passive

There are two main types of pickups: active and passive. Active pickups are less common than passive ones and require an external power source, usually a 9V battery. This is because active pickups have fewer coil windings and instead use an internal preamp to boost the signal. The preamp allows for a wider variety of tones and greater manipulation of the frequencies, making active pickups popular for high-gain, distorted tones. They tend to have very high output and, due to the reduced windings, pick up less electromagnetic interference. Active pickups are typically brighter than passive pickups, but they can sometimes sound brittle on a clean amp setting and don't always capture the player's dynamics as effectively.

Passive pickups, on the other hand, rely solely on the coil windings to transfer the signal from the magnetic field to the guitar's output. This results in a more natural portrayal of the player's dynamics, producing a warmer, more organic tone compared to active pickups. Passive pickups also have the advantage of not requiring batteries, saving money and time on replacements.



Position

The position of the pickup plays a significant role in shaping the tone. By position, we mean where the pickup is located—close to the bridge, the neck, or somewhere in between—how close it is to the strings, and the angle at which it is set (whether offset or parallel to the strings). These factors can greatly influence the sound produced.

Different parts of the string have varying tension. For instance, it's easier to move a string in the middle of the neck than near the bridge or nut. When the pickup is placed closer to the bridge, the string has more tension and requires more energy to move from its resting position. This results in more high frequencies being captured by the pickup. Conversely, when the pickup is closer to the neck, the string tension is lower, requiring less energy to move the string. This allows more low-end frequencies to be picked up.

The distance between the pickup and the strings also affects the signal strength. The closer the pickup is to the strings, the stronger the signal, as it's nearer to the source of the vibrations. However, if the pickup is too close, the magnet can pull on the string, dampening its vibrations and reducing sustain.

Finally, the angle of the pickup can impact the signal across different strings. For example, on a 6-string guitar, if the pole pieces for the lower strings (low E, A, D) are closer to the neck, more low-end frequencies will be captured for those strings. On the other hand, if the pole pieces for the higher strings (G, B, high e) are closer to the bridge, more high-end frequencies will be captured.



Wax Potting

Wax potting is the process of dipping a fully assembled pickup into hot wax and allowing it to harden. The magnet, pole pieces, and conductor are now completely encased in wax, this treatment helps reduce microphonic feedback and insulates the pickup from unwanted electromagnetic interference. Wax potting is very common in modern pickups and is essentially a requirement for any pickup designed for high-gain or heavily distorted tones.



How To Chose

With all these factors, choosing the right pickup can seem daunting, but it ultimately comes down to personal preference. While there's nothing wrong with chasing your guitar hero's tone, remember that you are your own player, and what works for someone else might not work for you. Take the time to explore, listen, and try as many pickups as you can. If you admire a certain guitarist's sound, research the pickups they use, check out the manufacturer's descriptions, and see how they align with your own tonal goals.
